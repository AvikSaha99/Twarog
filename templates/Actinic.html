Actinic Keratosis

Actinic Keratosis (AK), also known as solar keratosis, is a common skin condition caused by long-term exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation. AK appears as rough, scaly patches on sun-exposed areas of the skin, such as the face, ears, neck, scalp, chest, and hands. While AK itself is not cancerous, it is considered a precancerous condition that can develop into squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) if left untreated. Early detection and management are crucial to prevent progression to skin cancer.

Actinic Keratosis is characterized by the formation of rough, scaly, or crusty patches on the skin. These patches are often small, ranging from a few millimeters to several centimeters in diameter, and can be pink, red, brown, or skin-colored. AK lesions may feel dry or rough to the touch and can sometimes itch or become tender.

Diagnosis typically involves a physical examination by a dermatologist, who may use a dermatoscope to examine the lesions more closely. In some cases, a skin biopsy may be performed to rule out squamous cell carcinoma or other skin conditions.
